Seriously â€” but not catastrophically. That framing matters. The Structural Problem

On February 5, 2026 â€” exactly 23 days ago â€” the New START treaty between the US and Russia expired. This was the last binding nuclear arms control agreement between the world's two largest nuclear arsenals. With it went inspection regimes, verification mechanisms, and data-sharing protocols that have constrained both sides' worst-case assumptions since the early 1970s. The UN Secretary-General called the lapse "a grave moment for international peace and security." When verification disappears, worst-case assumptions fill the vacuum, and worst-case assumptions are exactly what lead to miscalculation.

Layer on top of this: Russia has spent three years making lightly veiled nuclear threats over Ukraine, and has developed and deployed new short-range "non-strategic" tactical nuclear weapons specifically designed for battlefield use at a scale below the threshold that would trigger full mutual retaliation. The US has responded by developing its own short-range nuclear cruise missiles. Both sides are simultaneously modernising all three legs of their nuclear triads. China will have 1,000 warheads by 2030. North Korea tested a new ICBM with a hypersonic delivery vehicle in 2025. The Bulletin of Atomic Scientists moved the Doomsday Clock to 89 seconds to midnight this January â€” the closest it has ever been.

The Iran conflict adds a specific new dimension to this background radiation of nuclear risk: uncertainty about fissile material. The Bulletin of Atomic Scientists noted that after the June 2025 US-Israel strikes, the fate of more than 400 kilograms of uranium enriched to 60% â€” enough for several nuclear weapons â€” became unclear. No IAEA inspector has visited Iranian nuclear sites since June. Today's strikes have struck additional nuclear-adjacent infrastructure. The material is there. Its location is not confirmed.

The Two Specific Nuclear Pathways Worth Taking Seriously

The Iran pathway is the less likely of the two but the most emotionally vivid. As of mid-February 2026, the Institute for Science and International Security estimated the probability of Iran building nuclear weapons at around 50%, or "even a little lower." That was before today's strikes further damaged its leadership and military infrastructure. A cornered regime with no leadership coherence, abundant enriched material, and a survival imperative is the highest-risk scenario for Iranian nuclear breakout â€” not because decision-making becomes easier, but because it becomes less predictable. If Iran builds a bomb, the target would almost certainly be Israel, not Western Europe or the US. Israel's Samson Option doctrine â€” believed to involve several hundred nuclear warheads â€” means a nuclear exchange in the Levant rather than a global conflagration, which is catastrophic for those affected but not an extinction-level event for the rest of the world.

The superpower pathway is lower probability but has much wider blast radius. It requires the following cascade: the Iran conflict drags on; Russia judges the moment optimal to escalate in Ukraine or the Baltics; a NATO conventional defence fails or proves insufficient; Russia uses a tactical nuclear weapon to break NATO's will, as Putin has explicitly threatened; and then the question of NATO's nuclear response falls to leadership that has never faced this decision. Every step in this chain has strong countervailing pressures. But every step has also moved measurably closer in the past 24 months.
